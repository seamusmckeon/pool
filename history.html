THE DROUGHT OF 1976-77
ALIGNED WITH THE RISE
OF SKATEBOARDING.



When we think of drought, we think of devastation to crops and lack of natural resources, but we must appreciate what drought has brought us: pool skating. This two-year devastation accounted for the 1st and 4th driest years in California history. So in effort to conserve water, swimming pools were being emptied all throughout Southern California. And with
that, emerging skaters like Tony Alva, Steve Olson, and the late Jay Adams were jumping fences and skating empty pools.


At the time, these kids were considered criminals. Now, they’re pioneers in skateboarding. But
no one was better known for their dedication to the cause quite like Steve Alba. Known as the Godfather of Pool Skating, he was the originator of finding pools, cleaning pools, and shredding
until the cops came. “The generation that I grew up skating in was one that already had skate-
parks established, but Steve Alba came from the true empty swimming pool era,” said Tony Hawk.
“So he was the one hopping fences, draining pools, and things like that.”

Of course these great people made pool skating happen, but what if California weren’t in a
severe drought back then? We might not have truly appreciated how great pools are for riding skateboards. We could have completely lost out on an entire category of skating. Furthermore,
pool skating assisted the evolution of vert and big air skating, ways of riding that we simply may
not have known about had it not been for a ragtag bunch of kids from Dogtown and an unfortun-
ate drought that ravaged California in the late 1970s. We should all be grateful.
